=== NDIS AUDITOR RUBRIC (SOURCE OF TRUTH) ===
Cenaris NDIS Compliance Source of Truth

Plain-English Explanation of Every NDIS Requirement

Audience: Non-NDIS developers, product designers, founders, compliance analysts
Purpose: Explain what each NDIS requirement actually means, why it exists, and what good vs bad evidence looks like in the real world.

How to Read This Document

For every NDIS requirement, we explain:

Outcome – What the NDIS is trying to protect or achieve

Indicator (Plain English) – What the provider must be able to show

Why this matters – The risk this requirement exists to prevent

Good evidence looks like – What auditors expect to see

Bad or weak evidence looks like – Common failure patterns

Typical evidence examples – Real artefacts providers use

This document is intentionally repetitive and explicit. That is by design.

VERIFICATION MODULE

(Entry-level safeguards for lower-risk providers)

VER-HRM-QI-01 — Worker suitability and records

Outcome

Participants are supported by people who are legally allowed to work, appropriately screened, and suitable for their role.

Indicator (Plain English)

The provider keeps proper records showing who each worker is, that they are allowed to work in Australia, and that required background checks and qualifications are in place.

Why this matters

People with disability are vulnerable to abuse and neglect if unsuitable people are allowed to work unchecked.

Good evidence looks like

Every worker has a complete personnel file

Required checks are current and verified

Records are easy to retrieve

Bad or weak evidence looks like

Some staff files missing documents

Expired police checks

Records spread across emails or personal drives

Typical evidence examples

Passport or ID copy

Right-to-work check

Police check

Qualification certificates

Central HR register

VER-HRM-QI-02 — NDIS Worker Orientation and training

Outcome

Workers understand their obligations to participants and the NDIS.

Indicator (Plain English)

Workers complete mandatory NDIS induction training and the provider keeps evidence of this.

Why this matters

Workers cannot protect participant rights if they don’t understand them.

Good evidence looks like

Training completed before independent work

Completion certificates stored centrally

Bad or weak evidence looks like

“We tell them verbally”

Training assumed but not recorded

Typical evidence examples

NDIS Worker Orientation certificates

Training register

VER-HRM-QI-03 — Infection prevention training

Outcome

Participants are protected from preventable infections.

Indicator (Plain English)

Workers are trained in basic infection prevention, including hygiene and standard precautions.

Why this matters

Poor infection control can cause serious harm, especially to vulnerable people.

Good evidence looks like

Infection control training completed by staff

Training refreshed periodically

Bad or weak evidence looks like

Policy exists but staff not trained

Typical evidence examples

Infection control training records

Induction checklist

Outcome

Participants’ legal and human rights are respected, protected and promoted in all supports.

Indicator (Plain English)

Why this matters

People with disability experience higher rates of rights violations when decisions prioritise convenience or risk avoidance over dignity and autonomy.

SUPPLEMENTARY MODULES — HIGH-RISK & SPECIALIST SUPPORTS

(Applies only when providers deliver specific high-risk or specialist supports)

These modules exist because some supports carry much higher risk. The NDIS expects extra safeguards, higher skills, and stronger evidence.

SUPPLEMENTARY — HIGH INTENSITY DAILY PERSONAL ACTIVITIES (HIDPA)

What this module is about (plain English)

Some participants need complex health-related supports delivered by disability workers (not hospitals). When this happens, the risk of serious harm increases.

This module ensures:

clinical oversight exists

workers are specifically trained

risks and emergencies are planned for

HIDPA-QI-01 — Participant-specific clinical plans

Outcome

Participants receive complex personal supports that are safe and appropriate.

Indicator (Plain English)

Each participant has a detailed, up-to-date plan written or reviewed by a qualified health professional.

Why this matters

Generic care instructions lead to serious medical errors.

Good evidence looks like

Participant-specific clinical plans

Regular professional reviews

Bad or weak evidence looks like

Generic instructions used for multiple people

Plans not reviewed by a qualified clinician

Typical evidence examples

Bowel care plans

Enteral feeding plans

Catheter care plans

HIDPA-QI-02 — Risk and emergency management

Outcome

Workers can respond safely if something goes wrong.

Indicator (Plain English)

Plans clearly describe risks, warning signs, and what to do in an emergency.

Why this matters

Delayed or incorrect responses can be fatal.

Good evidence looks like

Clear escalation instructions

Emergency responses practiced

Bad or weak evidence looks like

“Call 000” with no additional guidance

Typical evidence examples

Emergency flowcharts

Escalation protocols

HIDPA-QI-03 — Worker training and competency

Outcome

Workers are capable of delivering complex supports safely.

Indicator (Plain English)

Workers receive participant-specific training and are assessed as competent.

Why this matters

General disability training is not enough for medical tasks.

Good evidence looks like

Training linked to each participant’s needs

Competency sign-offs

Bad or weak evidence looks like

One-off group training only

Typical evidence examples

Skills checklists

Competency assessments
